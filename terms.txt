# Psychology
Cognition: Mental processes involved in thinking, memory, language, and problem solving.
Perception: The process of organizing and interpreting sensory information.
Attention: The selective focus of mental resources on certain information.
Working memory: A short-term system used to hold and manipulate information.
Long-term memory: Relatively permanent storage of information over extended time.
Classical conditioning: Learning through association between two stimuli.
Operant conditioning: Learning in which behavior is shaped by consequences.
Reinforcement: Any consequence that increases the likelihood of a behavior.
Punishment: Any consequence that decreases the likelihood of a behavior.
Extinction: The weakening of a learned response when reinforcement stops.
Generalization: Responding similarly to stimuli that resemble the original cue.
Discrimination: Learning to respond differently to distinct stimuli.
Observational learning: Learning by watching and imitating others.
Cognitive bias: A systematic pattern of deviation from rational judgment.
Confirmation bias: Tendency to seek or interpret information that supports existing beliefs.
Motivation: Internal and external forces that initiate and direct behavior.
Intrinsic motivation: Doing an activity for its inherent interest or satisfaction.
Extrinsic motivation: Doing an activity to obtain external rewards or avoid penalties.
Emotion: A complex psychological state involving feelings, thoughts, and physiology.
Stress: A response to demands or threats that challenge coping resources.
Resilience: Capacity to adapt and recover from adversity.
Personality: Enduring patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving.
Trait: A stable characteristic that influences behavior across situations.
Temperament: Biologically based tendencies in emotional and behavioral style.
Attachment: Emotional bond between people, especially between infant and caregiver.
Self-efficacy: Belief in ones ability to succeed at specific tasks.
Mindfulness: Nonjudgmental awareness of present-moment experience.
Neuroplasticity: The brains ability to reorganize structure and function.
Executive function: Higher-order control processes such as planning and inhibition.
Metacognition: Awareness and regulation of ones own thinking processes.

# Food
Cuisine: A style of cooking associated with a culture or region.
Ingredient: Any food item used to prepare a dish.
Recipe: A set of instructions for preparing a food dish.
Boil: To cook food in liquid at its boiling point.
Simmer: To cook gently in liquid just below boiling.
Saute: To cook quickly in a small amount of fat over relatively high heat.
Roast: To cook food with dry heat in an oven.
Bake: To cook by surrounding food with dry heat, usually in an oven.
Grill: To cook over direct radiant heat on a grate.
Steam: To cook using vapor from boiling water.
Blanch: To briefly cook food in boiling water, then cool rapidly.
Fermentation: Transformation of food by microorganisms such as yeast or bacteria.
Marinate: To soak food in a seasoned liquid before cooking.
Seasoning: Ingredients added to enhance flavor, such as salt, herbs, or spices.
Umami: Savory taste associated with glutamates and certain proteins.
Acidity: Sourness in food that brightens flavor and balances richness.
Texture: Physical feel of food, such as crisp, tender, or creamy.
Aroma: Smell of food that contributes strongly to flavor perception.
Nutrient: A substance in food needed for growth, health, and energy.
Protein: Macronutrient made of amino acids used for structure and function.
Carbohydrate: Macronutrient that provides a primary source of energy.
Fat: Macronutrient important for energy storage, cell function, and absorption.
Fiber: Indigestible plant material that supports digestive health.
Vitamin: Organic compound needed in small amounts for normal metabolism.
Mineral: Inorganic nutrient required for body structure and function.
Calorie: Unit of energy used to describe food energy content.
Portion: The amount of food served or consumed at one time.
Food safety: Practices that prevent foodborne illness and contamination.
Cross-contamination: Transfer of harmful microorganisms between foods or surfaces.
Shelf life: Length of time a food remains safe and acceptable to eat.

# Travel
Passport: Official government document used for international travel and identity.
Visa: Authorization allowing entry, stay, or transit in another country.
Itinerary: Planned route and schedule for a trip.
Layover: A stop between connecting flights before the final destination.
Jet lag: Temporary sleep disruption caused by crossing time zones.
Exchange rate: Value of one currency compared with another currency.
Customs: Border process for declaring goods entering a country.
Immigration: Border control process that verifies entry eligibility.
Timezone: A region that uses a uniform standard time.
UTC: Coordinated Universal Time, the global reference standard for time.
World heritage site: Place recognized for outstanding cultural or natural value.
Capital city: The city where a countrys central government is based.
Currency: The official system of money used in a country.
Vatican City: The smallest sovereign country in the world by area and population.
Tokyo: The capital city of Japan.
Ottawa: The capital city of Canada.
Canberra: The capital city of Australia.
Brasilia: The capital city of Brazil.
New Delhi: The capital city of India.
Cairo: The capital city of Egypt.
Yen: The official currency of Japan.
Euro: Shared currency used by many European Union member states.
Pound sterling: The official currency of the United Kingdom.
Canadian dollar: The official currency of Canada.
Australian dollar: The official currency of Australia.
Brazilian real: The official currency of Brazil.
Indian rupee: The official currency of India.
Egyptian pound: The official currency of Egypt.
Swiss franc: The official currency of Switzerland and Liechtenstein.
South African rand: The official currency of South Africa.

# Biology
Cell: The basic structural and functional unit of life.
Nucleus: Membrane-bound organelle that stores DNA in eukaryotic cells.
Mitochondrion: Organelle that generates most ATP through cellular respiration.
Ribosome: Cellular structure where proteins are assembled.
Cell membrane: Selectively permeable boundary that surrounds the cell.
Cytoplasm: Gel-like interior of a cell outside the nucleus.
DNA: Molecule that stores hereditary information.
Gene: Segment of DNA associated with a functional product.
Chromosome: DNA-protein structure carrying many genes.
Allele: Alternative form of a gene at a specific locus.
Protein: Polymer of amino acids with structural and functional roles.
Enzyme: Biological catalyst that speeds chemical reactions.
Photosynthesis: Process that converts light energy into chemical energy.
Cellular respiration: Process by which cells produce ATP from nutrients.
Homeostasis: Maintenance of stable internal conditions.
Tissue: Group of similar cells performing a common function.
Organ: Structure made of multiple tissues with specialized functions.
Organ system: Group of organs working together for major body functions.
Neuron: Cell specialized for rapid electrical and chemical signaling.
Hormone: Chemical messenger released to regulate target cells.
Immune system: Network that protects the body from pathogens.
Antibody: Immune protein that binds specific antigens.
Pathogen: Disease-causing biological agent.
Evolution: Change in inherited traits across generations.
Natural selection: Differential survival and reproduction based on heritable variation.
Adaptation: Heritable trait that increases fitness in an environment.
Ecosystem: Community of organisms interacting with the physical environment.
Producer: Organism that makes its own food, often via photosynthesis.
Consumer: Organism that obtains energy by eating other organisms.
Decomposer: Organism that breaks down dead matter and recycles nutrients.
