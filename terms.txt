# World Geography
Equator: The imaginary line at zero degrees latitude that circles Earth midway between the poles.
Prime Meridian: The reference line at zero degrees longitude that passes through Greenwich, England.
Latitude: The angular distance north or south of the equator measured in degrees.
Longitude: The angular distance east or west of the Prime Meridian measured in degrees.
Hemisphere: One half of Earth, such as the Northern, Southern, Eastern, or Western half.
Continent: A large continuous landmass recognized as one of Earths major regions.
Archipelago: A chain or group of islands located near one another.
Peninsula: Land nearly surrounded by water but connected to a larger land area.
Isthmus: A narrow strip of land connecting two larger land areas.
Delta: A landform of deposited sediment at the mouth of a river.
Fjord: A long, narrow sea inlet carved by glaciers and bordered by steep slopes.
Plateau: An area of high, relatively flat land.
Savanna: Grassland ecosystem with scattered trees and distinct wet and dry seasons.
Tundra: Cold, treeless biome with short growing seasons and frozen subsoil.
Taiga: Northern coniferous forest biome also called boreal forest.
Monsoon: Seasonal wind pattern that often brings heavy rainfall.
Rain Shadow: A dry region on the leeward side of a mountain range.
Watershed: Land area that drains precipitation to a common river, lake, or ocean outlet.
Tributary: A smaller stream or river that flows into a larger river.
Estuary: A coastal zone where freshwater from rivers mixes with seawater.
Strait: A narrow waterway connecting two larger bodies of water.
Gulf: A large inlet of sea partly enclosed by land.
Bay: A broad inlet of water where the land curves inward.
Atoll: Ring-shaped coral island or reef surrounding a lagoon.
Lagoon: A shallow body of water separated from a larger body by a barrier.
Glacier: A persistent, moving mass of compacted ice formed from snow.
Permafrost: Ground that remains frozen for at least two consecutive years.
Oasis: A fertile area in a desert where water is naturally available.
Desertification: The process by which fertile land becomes desert.
Urbanization: The growth of cities as populations concentrate in urban areas.

# Basic Astronomy
Astronomy: The scientific study of objects and phenomena beyond Earths atmosphere.
Solar System: The Sun and all objects bound to it by gravity.
Star: A luminous sphere of plasma powered by nuclear fusion.
Planet: A large body that orbits a star and has cleared its orbital neighborhood.
Dwarf Planet: A body that orbits the Sun and is nearly round but has not cleared its orbit.
Asteroid: A small rocky object orbiting the Sun.
Comet: An icy body that develops a glowing coma and tail when near the Sun.
Meteoroid: A small rocky or metallic body traveling through space.
Meteor: The streak of light seen when a meteoroid burns in Earths atmosphere.
Meteorite: A meteoroid fragment that survives the atmosphere and reaches the ground.
Orbit: The curved path of one object around another due to gravity.
Rotation: The spinning of an object around its own axis.
Revolution: The motion of one body around another body.
Axis: An imaginary line around which a body rotates.
Ellipse: An oval-shaped orbit with two focal points.
Light Year: The distance light travels in one year in a vacuum.
Astronomical Unit: The average distance between Earth and the Sun.
Galaxy: A massive system of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter bound by gravity.
Milky Way: The galaxy that contains Earth and our solar system.
Nebula: A cloud of gas and dust in space.
Supernova: A powerful stellar explosion near the end of a stars life cycle.
Black Hole: A region of space where gravity is so strong that light cannot escape.
Red Giant: A large, cool, luminous star in a late stage of stellar evolution.
White Dwarf: A small, dense stellar remnant left after a low-mass star sheds outer layers.
Neutron Star: An extremely dense stellar remnant composed mostly of neutrons.
Eclipse: An event where one celestial body moves into the shadow of another.
Solstice: The time when the Suns path reaches its northernmost or southernmost point.
Equinox: The time when day and night are nearly equal in length worldwide.
Constellation: A recognized pattern of stars forming a named region of the sky.
Exoplanet: A planet that orbits a star outside our solar system.

# Foundations of Biology
Cell: The basic structural and functional unit of life.
Nucleus: The membrane-bound organelle that contains DNA in eukaryotic cells.
Mitochondrion: The organelle that produces most cellular ATP through respiration.
Ribosome: The cellular structure that assembles proteins from amino acids.
Cell Membrane: The selectively permeable boundary surrounding a cell.
Cytoplasm: The gel-like contents inside a cell excluding the nucleus.
DNA: The molecule that stores hereditary genetic information.
Gene: A segment of DNA that contributes to a functional product.
Chromosome: A DNA-protein structure carrying many genes.
Allele: A variant form of a gene at a specific locus.
Protein: A polymer of amino acids that performs structural and functional roles.
Enzyme: A biological catalyst that speeds up chemical reactions.
Photosynthesis: The process by which plants convert light energy into chemical energy.
Cellular Respiration: The process of extracting energy from molecules to produce ATP.
Homeostasis: The maintenance of stable internal conditions in an organism.
Tissue: A group of similar cells working together for a specific function.
Organ: A structure composed of multiple tissues performing specialized functions.
Organ System: A group of organs that work together to support life processes.
Neuron: A specialized cell that transmits electrical and chemical signals.
Hormone: A signaling molecule released to regulate distant target cells.
Immune System: The network of cells and organs that defends against pathogens.
Antibody: A protein produced by B cells that binds specific antigens.
Pathogen: A microorganism or agent that causes disease.
Evolution: The change in inherited traits of populations across generations.
Natural Selection: Differential survival and reproduction based on heritable traits.
Adaptation: A heritable trait that improves survival or reproduction in a given environment.
Ecosystem: A community of organisms interacting with their physical environment.
Producer: An organism that makes its own food, usually through photosynthesis.
Consumer: An organism that obtains energy by eating other organisms.
Decomposer: An organism that breaks down dead material and recycles nutrients.
