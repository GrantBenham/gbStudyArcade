# Cellular Biology
Ribosomes: Structures that synthesize new protein molecules
Endoplasmic reticulum: Network of membranes that transports newly synthesized proteins within the cell
Mitochondrion: Organelle that performs metabolic activities and provides usable energy for the cell
Motor neuron: Neuron that conducts impulses from the central nervous system to a muscle
Sensory neuron: Neuron specialized to detect a particular type of stimulation
Presynaptic terminal: Swelling at the end of an axon that releases neurotransmitters
Functional explanation: Explanation of why a structure or behavior evolved as it did
Neurons: Cells specialized to receive information and transmit it to other cells
Membrane: Structure that separates the inside of the cell from the outside environment
Nucleus: Structure that contains the chromosomes
Axon: Fiber that carries an impulse away from the cell body to other cells
Cell body or soma: Part of the neuron containing the nucleus, ribosomes, and mitochondria
Dendritic spines: Short outgrowths on dendrites that increase surface area for synapses
Dendrites: Branching fibers that receive input from other neurons
Nodes of Ranvier: Gaps between sections of myelin along an axon
Myelin sheath: Insulating covering around certain axons
Afferent axon: Axon that brings information into a structure
Efferent axon: Axon that carries information away from a structure
Interneuron or intrinsic neuron: Neuron whose dendrites and axon are entirely contained within one structure
Glia: Nonneuronal cells that support, nourish, and protect neurons
Astrocytes: Star-shaped glial cells
Microglia: Glial cells that act as part of the immune system in the nervous system
Oligodendrocytes and Schwann cells: Glial cells that form myelin sheaths around axons
Radial glia: Glial cells that guide neuron migration during embryonic development
Blood–brain barrier: Mechanism that keeps many chemicals in the blood from entering the brain
Polarization: Difference in electrical charge between the inside and outside of a cell membrane
Resting potential: Stable negative electrical charge of a neuron when it is not firing
Selectively permeable: Permitting some chemicals to pass through more freely than others
Sodium–potassium pump: Protein complex that moves sodium ions out of the cell and potassium ions in
Concentration gradient: Difference in the distribution of ions across a membrane
Action potentials: Rapid depolarizations that travel along an axon
Hyperpolarization: Increase in the membrane's negative charge beyond the resting level
Threshold: Membrane voltage at which an action potential is triggered
All-or-none law: Principle that an action potential occurs fully or not at all once threshold is reached
Voltage-gated channels: Channels that open or close depending on membrane voltage
Local anesthetic: Drug that blocks sodium channels and prevents action potentials
Action potential propagation: Transmission of an action potential down an axon
Absolute refractory period: Time when a neuron cannot generate another action potential
Relative refractory period: Time when only a stronger-than-usual stimulus can trigger an action potential
Saltatory conduction: Conduction in which the impulse appears to jump from node to node along a myelinated axon
Biological psychology: Study of the physiological, evolutionary, and developmental mechanisms of behavior and experience
Dualism: View that mind and body are different kinds of substance
Mind–body problem: Question of how and why brain activity is accompanied by conscious experience
Monism: View that mental activity and brain activity are inseparable
Physiological explanation: Explanation that relates behavior to activity of the brain and other organs
Ontogenetic explanation: Explanation of how a structure or behavior develops within an individual
Evolutionary explanation: Explanation of the evolutionary history of a structure or behavior

# Synapses
EPSP: Graded depolarization of a postsynaptic membrane
IPSP: Temporary hyperpolarization of a postsynaptic membrane
Postsynaptic neuron: Neuron that receives transmission from another neuron
Presynaptic neuron: Neuron that delivers transmission to another neuron
Reflex arc: Neural circuit from sensory neuron to muscle response
Reflexes: Automatic muscular responses to stimuli
Spatial summation: Combined effect of activity from two or more synapses on one neuron
Spontaneous firing rate: Periodic production of action potentials without synaptic input
Synapse: Specialized junction where one neuron communicates with another cell
Temporal summation: Cumulative effect of repeated input at a single synapse over a brief time
Acetylcholine: Neurotransmitter released by many neurons, including those that activate muscles
Amino acids: One major class of neurotransmitters
Amphetamine: Drug that blocks reuptake of dopamine and certain other neurotransmitters
Anterior pituitary: Glandular portion of the pituitary gland
Autoreceptors: Receptors that respond to a neuron's own transmitter release by reducing further synthesis and release
Cannabinoids: Chemicals related to THC
Catecholamines: Chemicals that contain a catechol group and an amine group
Cocaine: Drug that blocks reuptake of dopamine
Endocrine glands: Glands that produce hormones
Exocytosis: Release of neurotransmitter from the presynaptic neuron into the synaptic cleft
G protein: Protein coupled to guanosine triphosphate (GTP) that helps produce intracellular effects
Gap junction: Direct connection between cells that allows electrical transmission
Gases: Class of neurotransmitters that includes nitric oxide
Hallucinogenic drugs: Drugs that distort perception
Hormone: Chemical secreted by cells in one part of the body and carried by the blood to affect other cells
Ionotropic effects: Rapid synaptic effects produced by opening membrane channels
Ligand-gated channels: Channels that open when a neurotransmitter binds to them
Metabotropic effects: Slower synaptic effects produced through a sequence of metabolic reactions
Methylphenidate: ADHD stimulant that increases dopamine activity by blocking dopamine reuptake
Monoamines: Neurotransmitters formed from certain amino acids
Neuromodulators: Chemicals that modify the effects of neurotransmitters
Neuropeptides: Neurotransmitters made of short chains of amino acids
Neurotransmitters: Chemicals released by neurons that influence other cells
Nitric oxide: Gaseous transmitter released by many small local neurons
Opiate drugs: Drugs derived from the opium poppy
Oxytocin: Hormone released by the posterior pituitary that is important for sexual and parental behaviors
Pituitary gland: Endocrine gland attached to the base of the hypothalamus
Posterior pituitary: Portion of the pituitary that releases hormones synthesized by the hypothalamus
Purines: Category of neurotransmitter-related chemicals that includes adenosine and related compounds
Releasing hormones: Hormones that stimulate or inhibit the release of other hormones
Reuptake: Reabsorption of a neurotransmitter by the presynaptic terminal
Second messenger: Intracellular chemical that carries a signal from the membrane to other parts of the cell
Synaptic cleft: Space between the presynaptic and postsynaptic cells
Transmitter-gated channels: Ion channels that open temporarily when a neurotransmitter binds to them
Transporters: Membrane proteins through which neurotransmitter reuptake occurs
Vasopressin: Hormone released by the posterior pituitary that raises blood pressure and helps the kidneys conserve water
Vesicles: Small membrane-enclosed packets filled with neurotransmitter molecules

Hindbrain: Posterior part of the brain
Hippocampus: Forebrain structure important for certain kinds of memory
Hypothalamus: Small forebrain area near the base of the brain, ventral to the thalamus
Inferior colliculus: Tectal structure that contributes to hearing

Meninges: Membranes that surround the brain and spinal cord
Midbrain: Middle part of the brain
Neuroanatomy: Study of the anatomy of the nervous system
Nucleus basalis: Forebrain structure on the ventral surface that sends axons to areas of the cerebral cortex

Peripheral nervous system (PNS): Nerves outside the brain and spinal cord
Pituitary gland: Endocrine gland attached to the base of the hypothalamus
Pons: Hindbrain structure anterior and ventral to the medulla
Somatic nervous system: Part of the PNS that carries sensory input to the CNS and motor output to muscles
Spinal cord: CNS structure that communicates with most of the body's sense organs and muscles
Substantia nigra: Midbrain structure that gives rise to a dopamine pathway
Superior colliculus: Tectal structure important for visual processing and orienting movements
Sympathetic nervous system: Division of the autonomic nervous system that prepares the body for vigorous activity
Tectum: Roof of the midbrain
Tegmentum: Intermediate level of the midbrain
Thalamus: Pair of forebrain structures near the center of the brain that relay sensory information to the cortex
Ventral: Toward the belly side
Ventricles: Four interconnected fluid-filled cavities within the brain
White matter: Nervous system tissue consisting mostly of myelinated axons
Anterior commissure: Small bundle of axons connecting parts of the two cerebral hemispheres
Binding problem: Question of how activity in different brain areas is combined into the perception of a single object
Central sulcus: Deep groove that separates the frontal lobe from the parietal lobe
Cerebral cortex: Outer layer of the cerebral hemispheres
Columns: Groups of cells arranged perpendicular to the cortical surface
Corpus callosum: Large bundle of axons connecting the two cerebral hemispheres
Default network: Brain areas that are most active during restful, internally directed thought
Frontal lobe: Portion of the cerebral cortex extending from the central sulcus to the front of the brain
Human Connectome Project: Project aimed at mapping long-distance brain connections
Klüver-Bucy syndrome: Behavioral disorder caused by temporal lobe damage
Laminae: Layers of cell bodies parallel to the cortical surface
Occipital lobe: Posterior part of the cerebral cortex
Parietal lobe: Portion of the cerebral cortex between the occipital lobe and the central sulcus
Postcentral gyrus: Cortical area just behind the central sulcus that is important for touch and body sensation
Precentral gyrus: Posterior portion of the frontal lobe specialized for movement control
Prefrontal cortex: Anterior part of the frontal lobe involved in planning, decision making, and guiding behavior
Prefrontal lobotomy: Surgical disconnection of the prefrontal cortex from the rest of the brain
Primates: Order of mammals that includes monkeys, apes, and humans
Temporal lobe: Lateral portion of the cerebral cortex near the temples
Temporoparietal junction: Region where the temporal and parietal lobes meet
Ablation: Removal of a brain area, usually by surgery
CT or CAT scan: Brain imaging method that uses X-rays to produce cross-sectional images
Electroencephalograph (EEG): Device that records electrical activity of the brain through electrodes on the scalp
Evoked potentials: Scalp-recorded brain responses to a stimulus
Evoked responses: Scalp-recorded brain responses to a stimulus
Functional MRI (fMRI): Imaging method that estimates brain activity from changes in blood oxygen use
Lesion: Damage to a structure
Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI): Imaging method that uses magnetic fields and radio waves to produce detailed images of the brain
Magnetoencephalograph (MEG): Device that measures magnetic fields generated by brain activity
Optogenetics: Method of inserting light-sensitive receptors into neurons and controlling them with light
Phrenology: Discredited attempt to relate skull shape to behavior
Positron-emission tomography (PET): Imaging method that maps brain activity using injected radioactive chemicals
Stereotaxic instrument: Device used for precise placement of electrodes or other instruments in the brain
Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS): Use of an intense magnetic field applied to the scalp to temporarily disrupt brain activity

Group selection: Idea that traits may spread because they benefit a group

Histones: Proteins around which DNA is wound
Homozygous: Having two identical alleles of a gene

Kin selection: Selection favoring a gene that benefits relatives who may share that gene
Lamarckian evolution: Discredited idea that acquired characteristics are inherited
Migrate: To move from one location to another during development
Monozygotic twins: Twins that develop from a single fertilized egg
